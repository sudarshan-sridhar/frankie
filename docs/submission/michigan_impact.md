# Michigan Impact

## The problem on the shop floor

Michigan still runs on its Tier 3 manufacturing base: the 200 to 2,000 person shops in Warren, Sterling Heights, Pontiac, Saginaw, and Grand Rapids that machine the brackets, fixtures, and subassemblies the big automakers and defense primes depend on. Those shops are losing their senior machinists to retirement faster than they can hire. When a 40 year veteran walks out the door, he takes with him the knowledge of which torque value won't strip an aluminum boss, which surface scratch makes a part scrap instead of rework, and which tool to grab for which job. That knowledge was never written down. It was taught at the bench, one apprentice at a time, and the apprentices stopped coming.

Meanwhile, every robotic helper on the market ships its own proprietary app. A foreman who pairs three different robots is asked to learn three different tablets, three different chat surfaces, three different log views. That's a tax Tier 3 cannot pay.

## What AURA does about it

**AURA is one app per shop, not one app per robot.** The foreman pairs Frankie today and adds whatever compatible robot ships tomorrow  -  same chat, same camera surface, same manual controls, same session history. The IBM Granite reasoning is shared across every robot the foreman connects.

The procedural knowledge that used to live in a senior machinist's head now lives in the shop's AURA install. A shop owner walks up to a paired robot with a defective part, shows it to the camera, and tells AURA in plain English why it's defective. A junior operator later asks AURA for an M6 and the right robot delivers it along with the senior machinist's warning about over-torquing on aluminum. The knowledge survives the retirement. The shop keeps running.

## Why this stays in Michigan

AURA is built for the shop that exists today, not the shop a Series B startup wishes existed. The mobile app is free to deploy. The reference robot  -  **Frankie** (Framework for Robotic Assistance, Networked Knowledge, Intelligent Engineering)  -  runs on under $1,000 of hardware: a 5-DOF arm, a Raspberry Pi 5, a phone bolted to a stand as the camera. There is no AWS bill, no enterprise integration team, no six month deployment. A shop foreman with a Home Depot account and a weekend can stand up the first robot. Adding a second compatible robot is a pairing step in the same app. That price point  -  and that low integration cost  -  is the difference between a tool Michigan's Tier 3 shops actually buy and a tool built for Fortune 500 plants in Texas.

## Mapping to HackMI judging criteria

AURA keeps value in Michigan four ways. It keeps the institutional knowledge of retiring machinists inside the shop instead of letting it walk out the door. It keeps junior operators viable by giving them a tireless mentor that levels them up faster. It keeps the work itself in Michigan by lowering the per-part cost enough that Tier 3 shops can keep bidding against offshore quotes. And it keeps the Michigan robotics ecosystem coherent  -  every new compatible robot extends the AURA install instead of fragmenting the foreman's attention across another app. That is what modernizing Michigan manufacturing looks like from the floor, not the boardroom.
