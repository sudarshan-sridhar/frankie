THE PROBLEM
Why utility pole risk is hard to see
Poles support the entire grid above ground — and what threatens them is everywhere around them.
Utility poles support critical electric and communications infrastructure across DTE's service territory. Their condition — and the condition of everything around them — directly determines whether the lights stay on, whether crews respond reactively or proactively, and whether outages last hours or days.
Traditional inspection cycles work on a schedule: a crew visits a section of territory on a rotation, looks at the poles, and moves on. This catches obvious problems, but it has two structural weaknesses. First, it misses early warning signs that only show up between visits — a tree that grew aggressively this season, soil saturation after a wet spring, a storm path that left invisible stress on the wood. Second, it can't prioritize. Every pole gets roughly the same amount of attention, regardless of whether it sits in a clear right-of-way or under a canopy of mature oaks next to a flood-prone creek.
The result is a maintenance posture that's reactive by design. Crews respond to outages after they happen. Vegetation gets trimmed on a rotation, not in response to actual risk. Capital flows to replacements after failures, not before. Reliability suffers, costs are higher than they need to be, and the data to do better exists — it's just not being assembled.
Meanwhile, the public data landscape has changed dramatically. Weather histories are downloadable. Satellite and aerial imagery is widely available. Vegetation indices, flood maps, soil data, terrain models — all open. The analytical tools to make sense of them — machine learning, computer vision, GIS platforms — are accessible to anyone with a laptop. The opportunity is to use them.
KEY INSIGHT
Traditional inspection cycles can miss early warning signs or fail to prioritize the highest-risk areas.

CONTEXT
What's changed — and why this challenge exists now
The conditions for a proactive, data-driven approach are finally in place.
DTE isn't asking teams to replace field inspections. The goal is to build a layer that complements them — something that tells crews where to look first, what to look for, and which conditions warrant intervention before the next storm hits. Three things make this challenge newly tractable in 2026:
DATA ACCESS
Public, free, layered
Weather history, satellite imagery, vegetation indices, soil and flood data, and open geographic datasets are now downloadable at the resolution utilities actually need.
TOOL MATURITY
ML & CV are accessible
Machine learning, computer vision, and GIS platforms that used to require specialist teams can now be assembled by small groups over a weekend.
OPERATIONAL PRESSURE
Reliability matters
Storm frequency, regulatory expectations, and customer tolerance for outages have all moved. Proactive maintenance is becoming a baseline expectation, not a stretch goal.

WHAT GOOD LOOKS LIKE
What a strong solution achieves
DTE is looking for outcomes, not specific architectures. Hit these and the implementation is up to you.
OUTCOMES TO AIM FOR
1
Risk is assessed at a meaningful unit
Pole-level or circuit-segment-level — granular enough to drive a work order, coarse enough to be tractable from public data.
2
The drivers of risk are visible
Not just a score, but what's behind it: vegetation encroachment, storm exposure, terrain, age proxies, environmental stress.
3
Recommendations are actionable
The output tells someone what to do — trim, inspect, reinforce, replace — not just where the problem is.
4
Decisions are explainable
Operations and field teams can see why an asset scored the way it did and trust the answer. No black boxes.
5
The approach scales
What works for a pilot circuit could plausibly work across DTE's full service territory without re-engineering.
THE TEST
Could a maintenance planner change their next week's schedule based on what your tool shows?
If yes, you've moved DTE from reactive to predictive. If no, you've built a research artifact — interesting, but not the goal here.

DATA & RESOURCES
What teams have to work with
DTE's challenge is built on public and synthetic data — no proprietary inputs required. Here's the landscape.
ENVIRONMENTAL & WEATHER DATA
Weather history and storm patterns — wind speed, ice events, lightning strikes
Long-term climate and precipitation records
Storm tracks and severity by region and date
Forecasts and seasonal patterns for predictive modeling
GEOSPATIAL & IMAGERY
Satellite imagery (Landsat, Sentinel, NAIP)
Aerial photography where available
Vegetation density and growth indicators (NDVI and similar)
Terrain, elevation, and slope data
Soil composition and flood-risk maps
Open infrastructure datasets and base maps
POLE & EQUIPMENT CONTEXT
Pole types, age bands, and material assumptions — proxy modeling where actual asset data is limited
Nearby vegetation proximity and growth trends
Environmental stress exposure derived from location
Synthetic data is fair game for filling gaps

EVALUATION & CONSTRAINTS
How DTE will evaluate solutions
Criteria are drawn directly from the challenge brief. Constraints are non-negotiable.
EVALUATION CRITERIA
IMPACT
Does the solution meaningfully improve prioritization of maintenance and investment?
INNOVATION
Use of public data, creative modeling, or emerging tools in a way that hasn't been done before.
ACCURACY & LOGIC
Soundness of the risk factors chosen and the scoring approach built on them.
USABILITY
Clear, actionable outputs that operational teams could actually use.
SCALABILITY
Plausibility of expanding the approach across the full service territory.
CONSTRAINTS
Use only publicly available or synthetic data
Design for incomplete and imperfect datasets
Prioritize explainability over black-box models
Focus on practical application, not theoretical perfection
